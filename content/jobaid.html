What Managers Need to Know About
Priority Entitlements and Appointments

REMEMBER! The Public Service Commission (PSC) Appointment Policy requires that deputy heads:

  • consider and assess persons with a priority entitlement before considering other candidates
  • respect requirements to administer priority entitlements as set out in the Priority Administration Directive

Key Points:

  • Priority entitlements apply to all organizations subject to the Public Service Employment Act.
  • Persons with a priority entitlement may demonstrate interest (self-refer) to be considered to any job being staffed or about
    to be staffed.
  • Appointments of persons with a priority entitlement are not subject to recourse.
  • Areas of selection in an appointment process do not apply to persons benefiting from a priority entitlement, even if the area of selection includes an employment equity (EE) requirement; however, if the appointment is made under an EE program, the person with the priority entitlement must meet that criterion.
  • Persons with a priority entitlement who are considered part of the area of selection in an internal appointment process
    can file a complaint to the Federal Public Sector Labour Relations and Employment Board of Canada, whether they were evaluated or not.
  • Even if they are not in the area of selection, persons with a priority entitlement have other recourses such as requests for investigation by the Public Service Commission or complaints to the Canadian Human Rights Commission.

Why hire a person with a priority entitlement?

  • Deputy heads recognize that there is both a legal and a corporate responsibility to assist indeterminate employees and former members of the Canadian Armed Forces, the RCMP or surviving spouses in coping with a change in life or employment.
  • It is one of the fastest ways to staff a position.
  • Persons with a priority entitlement need only meet the essential qualifications and the conditions of employment; in many cases they can simply be deployed.
  • Many persons with a priority entitlement have a corporate memory and are already adapted to federal government and departmental cultures.
  • Many persons with a priority entitlement have a shorter learning curve than new public servants and have completed the required and mandatory training in the areas of values and ethics, creating and maintaining a respectful workplace, health
    and safety, financial and HR delegations, and more.

Statutory Priorities
  1. 1. Canadian Armed Forces member medically released for reasons attributable to service

    Canadian Armed Forces (CAF) members medically released for reasons attributable to service

    Who:

    A person who was released from the CAF for medical reasons that Veterans Affairs Canada determines are attributable to service

    Highlights:

    Priority for appointment is given to these individuals over all other persons. This is the highest priority type under the Public Service Employment Act (PSEA).

    There is a qualifying period of five (5) years, within which they must be certified by a competent authority as being ready to return to work.

  2. 2. Surplus considered in home organization​

    Surplus employee

    Who:

    An employee who has been advised by the deputy head that their services are no longer required and before the layoff becomes effective

    Highlights:

    In the home organization, the employee is entitled to be appointed to another position under the deputy head’s jurisdiction ahead of all others except for those who have a priority entitlement as a Canadian Armed Forces member medically released for reasons attributable to service. (PSEA, section 40)

    Provisions for declaring an employee surplus are made in accordance with to the Workforce Adjustment Agreement Directive and Annexes in related collective agreements.

    The employee may be entitled to retraining as well as to salary protection
    and reinstatement if appointed to a lower-level position.

  3. 3. Leave of absence returnee / Leave of absence replacement

    Leave of absence returnee / Leave of absence replacement

    Who:

    An employee on leave of absence who is replaced, pursuant to the appointment or deployment of another person for an indeterminate period to the employee’s position

    or

    the employee who replaced the employee on leave of absence if that person returns to the position (PSEA, subsection 41(1))

    Highlights:

    The manager must decide whether to retain the employee returning from leave or the employee hired as a leave replacement.

    The employee who is not retained is the one who will have the priority entitlement.

    The decision on who to retain should be made at the same time as the decision to backfill the position on an indeterminate basis. Also, the decision should be communicated to the two employees before the appointment becomes effective.

    The employee may be eligible for a reinstatement priority entitlement
    if appointed to a lower-level position.

  4. 4. Person laid off

    Person laid off

    Who:

    Employee whose services are no longer required due to a lack of work, the discontinuance of a function, or the transfer of work or a function outside the public service

    Highlights:

    In addition to the provisions of the PSEA (Section 64(1)), the lay-off process is subject to other conditions and obligations specified in the Workforce Adjustment Agreement Directive (WFAD), and Annexes in related collective agreements.

    The employee may be entitled to retraining as well as salary protection and reinstatement if appointed to a lower-level position.

    The laid off person may participate in internal advertised processes as though they had not been laid off.

No order of consideration for appointments
Regulatory Priorities
  • Canadian Armed Forces member released for medical reasons not attributable to service ​

    Canadian Armed Forces (CAF) members released for medical reasons not attributable to service

    Who:

    CAF member who is released for medical reasons that are not attributable to service

    Highlights:

    There is a qualifying period of five (5) years within which the employee must be certified by a competent authority as being ready to return to work.

  • Employee who became disabled​

    Employee who became disabled​

    Who:

    Employee who becomes disabled and who, as a result of the disability, is no longer able to carry out the duties of their position

    Highlights:

    The employee has to qualify for disability compensation under a plan specified in the Public Service Employment Regulations.

    The entitlement continues even if, as a result of the person’s disability,
    they cease to be an employee.

    The home manager’s duty to accommodate continues to apply even if the employee becomes eligible for this priority entitlement.

    The employee may be entitled to other types of priority entitlements concurrently, as well as to reinstatement if appointed to a lower-level position.

  • Royal Canadian Mounted Police member discharged for medical reasons​

    Royal Canadian Mounted Police (RCMP) members discharged for medical reasons

    Who:

    A member of the RCMP who is discharged for medical reasons or a member of the Reserve of the RCMP who is discharged for medical reasons attributable to service.

    Highlights:

    There is a qualifying period of five (5) years within which the employee must be certified by a competent authority as being ready to return to work.

  • Surviving spouse common-law partner (the death is attributable to the performance of duties)​

    Surviving spouse common-law partner (the death is attributable to the performance of duties)​

    Who:

    Spouse or common-law partner following the death of an employee of the public service, the Canadian Armed Forces or the RCMP members and reserves

    Highlights:

    The death is attributable to the performance of duties.

    The priority for appointment applies to advertised external appointment processes.

    The spouse or common-law partner:

    • is not employed in the public service for an indeterminate period when the request is made
    • qualifies under a legislated compensation plan

    makes the request within two (2) years of qualifying for compensation

  • Relocation of spouse or common-law partner​

    Relocation of spouse or common-law partner

    Who:

    An employee who is on a leave of absence that has been granted as a result of the relocation of their spouse or common-law partner

    Highlights:

    Priority for appointment continues even if the employee is appointed to a specified period (term) position while on leave.

    If the employee’s position is backfilled indeterminately as a result of an appointment or deployment, the employee becomes entitled to another leave of absence priority entitlement effective on the date the position
    was backfilled.

    The employee may be eligible for reinstatement priority entitlement if appointed to a lower-level position.

  • Surplus considered in other organizations​

    Surplus employee

    Who:

    An employee who has been advised by the deputy head that their services are no longer required and before the layoff becomes effective

    Highlights:

    In other organizations, the employee is entitled to be appointed to any position ahead of all others after the statutory priorities have been considered. (Section 5 of the Public Service Employment Regulations (PSER))

    Provisions for declaring an employee surplus are made in accordance with to the Workforce Adjustment Agreement Directive and Annexes in related collective agreements.

    The employee may be entitled to retraining as well as to salary protection
    and reinstatement if appointed to a lower-level position.

  • Reinstatement

    Reinstatement to original level

    Who:

    An employee with a priority entitlement who is appointed or deployed to a position at a lower level to maintain employment

    Highlights:

    This priority for appointment applies to an employee who was appointed or deployed on an indeterminate basis to a lower-level position while holding one of the following types of priority entitlements:

    • Surplus
    • Leave of absence
    • Layoff
    • Employee who becomes disabled
    • Relocation of spouse /common-law partner

    The priority for appointment does not apply to a position that is at a higher level than the employee’s original position. However, they can still apply for
    a promotional appointment as a regular candidate.

To learn more about priority entitlements, consult the Public Service Commission’s Guide on Priority Entitlements.
